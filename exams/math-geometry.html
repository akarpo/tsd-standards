Instructions

  • Time limit: 120 minutes total. Part I (no-calculator) is intended to take about 45 minutes; Part II (calculator-permitted) the remaining 75 minutes.

Part I · No Calculator · Problems 1–4 · 40 pts · ~45 min
1. Rigid motions and congruence 8 points

Triangle \( ABC \) has vertices \( A(1, 2),\ B(5, 2),\ C(3, 5) \). Triangle \( A'B'C' \) has vertices \( A'(-2, 1),\ B'(-2, 5),\ C'(-5, 3) \).

  1. Show that \( \triangle ABC \cong \triangle A'B'C' \) by computing the lengths of all three pairs of corresponding sides. Use the distance formula and show your work.
  2. Describe a sequence of rigid motions that maps \( \triangle ABC \) onto \( \triangle A'B'C' \). Be specific (e.g., "rotation 90° counterclockwise about the origin, then translation by \(\langle a, b \rangle\)"). Verify the image of at least two vertices after each motion.
  3. Is the sequence in (b) unique? Give a one-sentence justification.
2. Similar triangles & parallel-line proportions 10 points

In \(\triangle ABC\), point \(D\) lies on \(\overline{AB}\) and point \(E\) lies on \(\overline{AC}\) with \(\overline{DE} \parallel \overline{BC}\). Given \( AD = 6 \), \( DB = 9 \), and \( AE = 4 \).

  1. Justify why \(\triangle ADE \sim \triangle ABC\). Cite the appropriate similarity postulate or theorem.
  2. Find \(EC\). Show the proportion you set up.
  3. Given \(BC = 25\), find \(DE\).
  4. The area of \(\triangle ADE\) is \(16\) cm\(^2\). Find the area of \(\triangle ABC\). Explain in one sentence why area scales as the square of the linear ratio.

4. Circles — chords, inscribed angles, and equations 12 points
  1. State the chord–chord power-of-a-point theorem: if chords \(\overline{AB}\) and \(\overline{CD}\) intersect at \(P\) inside a circle, then \(AP \cdot PB = CP \cdot PD\). Given \(AP = 4\), \(PB = 9\), \(CP = 6\), find \(PD\).
  2. An inscribed angle of a circle subtends an arc of measure 80°. Find the measure of the inscribed angle. State which theorem you used.
  3. From an external point \(T\), a tangent of length 12 and a secant are drawn to a circle. The secant intersects the circle at points \(A\) and \(B\) with \(A\) the nearer intersection and \(TA = 8\). Find \(AB\). Show your work using the tangent–secant relationship \( (TA + AB) \cdot TA = (\text{tangent})^2 \).
  4. Find the equation of the circle that passes through \((0,0)\), \((6,0)\), and \((0,8)\). Use either the general form \(x^2 + y^2 + Dx + Ey + F = 0\) and three equations, or recognize a special triangle. Show your work and write your final answer in standard form \((x - h)^2 + (y - k)^2 = r^2\).
Part II · Calculator Permitted · Problems 5–10 · 60 pts · ~75 min
5. Right-triangle trigonometry — surveying 10 points

A surveyor stands at point \(P\) on level ground and measures the angle of elevation to the top of a building as \(38°\). She walks 50 feet directly toward the building and measures a new angle of elevation of \(54°\). Let \(h\) be the height of the building and \(d\) be her distance from the building's base after walking.

  1. Set up two trigonometric equations relating \(h\), \(d\), and the given angles.
  2. Solve the system for \(h\) and \(d\). Round each to the nearest tenth of a foot. Show every algebraic step.
  3. Verify your answer by checking that \(\tan(54°) \cdot d \approx h\) within rounding tolerance.
  4. The surveyor mistakenly recorded the second angle as \(53°\) instead of \(54°\). Recompute \(h\) under the incorrect angle. By how many feet does the computed height differ? Round to the nearest tenth.
6. Coordinate geometry — quadrilateral classification 10 points

Let \(A = (-2, 1)\), \(B = (3, 4)\), \(C = (5, 0)\), \(D = (0, -3)\).

  1. Compute the lengths of all four sides \(\overline{AB}\), \(\overline{BC}\), \(\overline{CD}\), \(\overline{DA}\). Show your work.
  2. Compute the lengths of both diagonals \(\overline{AC}\) and \(\overline{BD}\).
  3. Compute the slopes of all four sides. Identify any pair of sides that is parallel and any pair that is perpendicular.
  4. Based on (a)–(c), classify quadrilateral \(ABCD\) as most specifically as possible (parallelogram, rectangle, rhombus, square, or none of these). Justify with the appropriate property or properties.
7. Volume of a composite solid — grain silo 10 points

A grain silo consists of a right circular cylinder of radius 8 ft and height 30 ft, capped by a hemispherical dome of radius 8 ft.

  1. Find the total volume of the silo (cylinder + dome) in cubic feet. Express the exact value (in terms of \(\pi\)) and the decimal value to the nearest cubic foot.
  2. Find the total external surface area (lateral surface of the cylinder + curved surface of the hemisphere; do not include the floor). Express both exactly and to the nearest square foot.
  3. The silo is filled with grain of mass density 50 lb/ft\(^3\). Find the total mass of grain in pounds. Round to the nearest pound.
  4. Suppose the silo is partially filled to a depth of 25 ft, measured from the floor (so only a portion of the cylinder is filled, none of the dome). What percent of the silo's total capacity is filled? Round to the nearest 0.1%.
8. Cavalieri's principle & cross-sections 8 points

A right square pyramid has height 12 cm and a square base of side 6 cm. A right circular cone has height 12 cm and a circular base of radius \(r\) cm.

  1. State Cavalieri's principle in your own words in one sentence. Then write the algebraic condition on \(r\) that, by Cavalieri's principle, makes the volumes of the two solids equal.
  2. Solve for \(r\) in (a). Give the exact value (in terms of \(\sqrt{\pi}\)) and a decimal to two places.
  3. Take a horizontal cross-section at height 4 cm above the base of each solid. Compute the area of each cross-section using a similarity argument; show how each cross-section's linear dimension scales with height.
9. Modeling with geometry — optimal cylindrical can 10 points

A standard 12-ounce beverage can is approximately a right circular cylinder of volume 355 cm\(^3\). The manufacturer wants to minimize the amount of aluminum (i.e., the total surface area: top + bottom + lateral) needed to produce each can.

  1. Let the can have radius \(r\) and height \(h\). Use the volume constraint to express \(h\) as a function of \(r\) only.
  2. Write the total surface area \(S\) as a function of \(r\) only.
  3. Numerically estimate the value of \(r\) that minimizes \(S\). Use the table below — fill in the missing values for \(S(r)\), then identify the approximate optimal \(r\) (round to one decimal place).

  4. For your optimal \(r\), compute \(h\) and the ratio \(h : 2r\). Comment in one sentence on the geometric significance of this ratio (compare to the typical real-world soda can, in which \(h : 2r\) is closer to 2).

Two hundred high-school students were classified by grade level (Junior or Senior) and by whether they participate in a varsity sport. The results are shown.

  1. If a student is selected at random, find \(P(S)\) and \(P(J)\).
  2. Find \(P(S \cap J)\), the probability that a randomly chosen student is a Junior who plays a varsity sport.
  3. Find \(P(S \mid J)\), the probability that a student plays a varsity sport given that the student is a Junior. Show the conditional-probability formula explicitly.
